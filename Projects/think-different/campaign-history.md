# Think Different — Campaign History

## Apple in Crisis: 1997

When Steve Jobs returned to Apple in July 1997 as interim CEO, the company was dying. Market share had cratered from roughly 14% in 1993 to below 3%. The company was burning cash at an alarming rate — by some accounts, roughly 90 days from insolvency. A succession of CEOs (John Sculley, Michael Spindler, Gil Amelio) had failed to reverse the slide. The product line was bloated, unfocused, and uninspiring. Apple's brand — once synonymous with creative rebellion — had become associated with decline.

Jobs understood that before he could fix the products (which would take time), he needed to fix the brand. Apple's customers — the designers, educators, filmmakers, and creative professionals who had stayed loyal — needed a reason to believe again. And the wider world needed to remember what Apple stood for.

He needed an ad campaign. Not a product campaign — Apple had nothing worth advertising yet — but a values campaign. A declaration of identity.

## The Agency: TBWA\Chiat\Day

Jobs turned to TBWA\Chiat\Day, the Los Angeles agency that had created Apple's legendary "1984" Super Bowl commercial. The relationship had been severed during the Sculley era, but Jobs brought them back immediately.

The key creatives:

- **Lee Clow** — CEO and Chief Creative Officer of TBWA\Chiat\Day. The senior creative leader who managed the relationship with Jobs with what Rob Siltanen later described as "the patience of a saint." Clow had overseen the "1984" spot and understood Apple's brand DNA deeply.
- **Rob Siltanen** — Creative Director and managing partner. Co-wrote the "Here's to the Crazy Ones" narration. His 2011 Forbes article provided the most detailed insider account of the campaign's creation.
- **Craig Tanimoto** — Art Director. Created the "Think different" slogan and the foundational visual concept of pairing it with black-and-white photographs of historical visionaries.
- **Ken Segall** — Creative Director. Co-wrote the narration with Siltanen. Later became known for convincing Jobs to name the iMac (the "i" prefix that defined a generation of Apple products).
- **Jessica Schulman Edelstein** — Art and Design Director. Researched, curated, and developed the visual identity of the print and outdoor campaign. Met weekly with Lee Clow and Steve Jobs to refine the look and feel.

## The Creative Spark

Craig Tanimoto's initial concept began in his notebook with a sketch for a billboard: a huge image of a boxy Mac with the phrase "This is not a box" — inspired by Rene Magritte's surrealist painting *The Treachery of Images* ("Ceci n'est pas une pipe"). The Magritte connection was apt: an artist who challenged people to see beyond the surface of things.

From there, Tanimoto progressed to sketching "unique and social outcasts" paired with the phrase **"Think Different."** His early drawings included Thomas Edison holding a lightbulb and Mahatma Gandhi, each with the Apple logo and the two-word slogan. He kept the concept secret from colleagues until the internal presentation, believing "ideas are so fragile."

When Lee Clow saw it, he declared: "This is the campaign, everyone is working on this one."

The slogan's grammatical unconventionality was intentional. "Think different" uses "different" as a noun or colloquial adverb — *think [about the] different*, or *think [in a way that is] different* — in the same register as "think big" or "think smart." Critics called it ungrammatical. Apple saw it as a statement that practiced what it preached: even the grammar refused to conform.

## "Here's to the Crazy Ones"

Rob Siltanen and Ken Segall drafted the narration that would become the campaign's soul. Siltanen drew inspiration from Robin Williams' speeches in *Dead Poets Society* — the passionate advocacy for seeing the world differently, for refusing to accept the ordinary.

The text, as aired:

> Here's to the crazy ones. The misfits. The rebels. The troublemakers. The round pegs in the square holes. The ones who see things differently. They're not fond of rules. And they have no respect for the status quo. You can quote them, disagree with them, glorify or vilify them. About the only thing you can't do is ignore them. Because they change things. They push the human race forward. And while some may see them as the crazy ones, we see genius. Because the people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do.

When the team first presented the concept to Jobs, his reaction was volcanic: "It sucks! I hate it! It's advertising agency ****! I thought you were going to write something like *Dead Poets Society*! This is crap!" Siltanen had drawn on exactly that inspiration — but Jobs didn't initially see the connection. In classic Jobs fashion, he later reversed course and embraced the campaign fully.

### The Narrator

Jobs wanted Robin Williams to narrate. Williams was known for refusing advertising work, and his wife reportedly declined to forward Jobs' call. Tom Hanks was also considered. The team ultimately chose **Richard Dreyfuss**, who was an Apple fan and accepted readily. Dreyfuss's warm, slightly gravelly delivery gave the narration a quality that was reverential without being pious — like a friend telling you about people he genuinely admired.

Jobs also recorded a version himself. His reading included additional lines not in the aired version: "They invent. They imagine. They heal. They explore. They create. They inspire. They push the human race forward." But Jobs refused to let his version air, believing people would think him an egomaniac and wouldn't hear the message. After recording, he reportedly said: "That's it, I'm out of here. This is a horrible idea."

Jobs' version was first played publicly at Apple's internal memorial for him in October 2011, nine years after the campaign ended and days after his death.

## The 17-Day Sprint

After Jobs approved the campaign, he gave the team **17 days** to complete everything — the 60-second television commercial and the full billboard/print rollout. For a campaign of this ambition and scope, the timeline was extraordinary. The team had to secure archival footage and photography, negotiate licensing rights for dozens of historical figures, produce the TV spot, and design and produce large-format print materials, all simultaneously.

## Securing the Rights

Jobs was personally involved in obtaining permission to use the likenesses, many of which had never appeared in advertising before. The families and estates had to trust that their loved ones' images would be treated with dignity — that this was celebration, not exploitation.

Jobs **flew to New York City** to visit Yoko Ono in person to secure the use of John Lennon's image. At an internal Apple meeting afterward, he said: "I got permission from Yoko Ono a few days ago to use John." He **personally called** the family of Jim Henson. Jobs noted that "these people, both living and dead, their estates, have felt so strongly about Apple" that they were willing to participate — a testament to Apple's enduring cultural credibility even at its lowest commercial point.

## The Television Commercial

The 60-second spot first aired on **September 28, 1997**. Over black-and-white archival footage of 17 iconic figures, Richard Dreyfuss delivered the "Crazy Ones" narration. The figures appeared in sequence: Einstein, Dylan, King, Branson, Lennon and Ono, Fuller, Edison, Ali, Turner, Callas, Gandhi, Earhart, Hitchcock, Graham, Henson with Kermit, Wright, Picasso. The spot closed with an unidentified young girl — Shaan Sahota — opening her eyes, followed by the Apple logo and "Think different."

No product was shown. No feature was described. No price was mentioned. The ad said nothing about computers. It said everything about what Apple believed.

A 30-second variant aired once during the **Seinfeld series finale**, replacing the closing shot of the young girl with Jerry Seinfeld.

## The Print and Poster Campaign

The print campaign was far more extensive than the television spot. Large-format (24×36 inch) black-and-white posters featured individual portraits with the Apple logo and "Think different" — nothing else. No copy, no product, no call to action. Just a face, a logo, and two words.

The posters were released in numbered sets:

**Set 1:** Amelia Earhart, Alfred Hitchcock, Pablo Picasso, Mahatma Gandhi, Thomas Edison

**Set 2:** Maria Callas, Martha Graham, Joan Baez, Ted Turner, 14th Dalai Lama (Dalai Lama poster created but never officially released — licensing issues and political sensitivity given China/Tibet relations)

**Set 3:** Jimi Hendrix, Miles Davis, Ansel Adams, Lucille Ball & Desi Arnaz, Bob Dylan (created but never officially released — licensing issues), Paul Rand

**Set 4:** Frank Sinatra, Richard Feynman, Jackie Robinson, Cesar Chavez

**Set 5 — "The Directors":** Charlie Chaplin, Francis Ford Coppola, Orson Welles, Frank Capra, John Huston (never officially released as a set — now highly sought by collectors)

Additional standalone posters featured Albert Einstein, Muhammad Ali, Jim Henson with Kermit, John Lennon and Yoko Ono, Nelson Mandela, Eleanor Roosevelt, Buzz Aldrin, Bill Bernbach, George Gershwin, Rosa Parks, James Watson, Jane Goodall, and Ron Howard.

A **Pixar promotional tie-in** poster featured Flik from *A Bug's Life* in the Think Different visual format.

### The Educator Series (2000)

A set of 10 smaller (11×17 inch) posters was distributed through Apple's education channels to schools: Einstein, Cesar Chavez, Jane Goodall, Picasso, Earhart, Miles Davis, Henson, Lennon and Ono, James Watson, and Gandhi. The selection emphasized figures whose stories could inspire students — scientists, activists, artists, and explorers.

## The Authorship Dispute

The question of who wrote "Here's to the Crazy Ones" became contentious after Jobs' death. Walter Isaacson's authorized biography (2011) gave significant credit to Jobs for shaping the narration text. Rob Siltanen responded with a detailed Forbes article in December 2011, documenting from his handwritten creative journals and saved files from 1997 that the core script — its opening, soul, and ending — remained largely unchanged from the agency's original draft.

Siltanen acknowledged that Jobs was deeply involved in every facet of Apple's business and marketing, but argued he was "far from the mastermind" behind the narration itself. The truth likely sits where creative collaborations usually do: the agency wrote it, Jobs pushed them to make it better, and the final product was shaped by the tension between them.

## Awards and Reception

- **1998 Emmy Award** for Best Commercial
- **2000 Grand Effie Award** for most effective campaign in America

The campaign's impact was immediate and profound. It didn't sell a single product — and it didn't need to. It rebuilt Apple's identity from the inside out. Employees who had been demoralized by years of decline suddenly had a banner to rally around. The creative community that had always been Apple's base saw that the company still understood them. And the broader public was reminded that Apple was — or aspired to be — something more than a computer company.

## Legacy and End

The Think Different campaign ran from **1997 to approximately 2002**, phased out around the arrival of the iMac G4. By then, it had done its work. Apple had a new identity, a new product line (iMac, iPod), and a new trajectory. The values campaign had bought time for the product revolution.

The campaign's influence extends far beyond Apple. It established the modern template for values-based brand advertising — the idea that a company could build loyalty not by describing what it makes, but by declaring what it believes. Every brand campaign since that ties itself to a cause or a worldview owes something to Think Different.

More personally, it was Jobs' manifesto. The people in those photographs — the rebels, the misfits, the troublemakers — were the people he most admired. The narration wasn't just about them. It was about how he saw himself and what he wanted Apple to be: a company crazy enough to think it could change the world. And then doing it.
