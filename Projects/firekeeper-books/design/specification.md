# Wisdom Library — Phase 1 Specification

**Phase:** 1 — Premium Illustrated Ebook Editions
**Classification:** Internal — Danny + Crumb/Tess
**Parent research:** [[side-hustle-v7-public-domain-wisdom-library]]

---

## 1. Mission

Make foundational human expression — philosophy, fiction, drama, poetry — beautiful and accessible again through premium illustrated digital editions of public domain texts. The unifying principle isn't genre — it's endurance and impact. From the Greek tragedies onward, fiction and drama represent a nearly endless well of material, and are arguably *more* suited to illustrated editions because of stronger visual anchor points (scenes, characters, settings vs. abstract philosophical themes). Monetization is the sustainability model, not the mission. Revenue validates that the work is reaching people.

---

## 2. Phase 1 Scope

Produce and publish beautifully designed, illustrated digital editions of public domain texts — spanning fiction, drama, poetry, and philosophy. Ebook only — audiobook and multilingual editions are deferred to Phase 2 and Phase 3 respectively. Fiction-first strategy: illustrations are the moat, and fiction maximizes that moat through concrete visual anchor points (characters, settings, dramatic scenes). First title is Frankenstein (1818 text), second is The Odyssey (timed to the Nolan film, July 17, 2026). The series identity and design system must accommodate all genres from the start.

### In Scope

- Source text acquisition and preparation (Project Gutenberg, Internet Archive)
- **Modernized translation adaptation** — update archaic language in public domain translations for contemporary readability while preserving meaning and tone
- Series design system (typography, color palette, layout templates, illustration style guide)
- AI-generated original illustrations (10+ per title, KDP requirement)
- Cover design (series-consistent, AI-generated with human art direction)
- Ebook formatting and production (epub/mobi)
- Publishing to KDP (Amazon), Kobo, Google Play, Apple Books (via aggregator) — **wide distribution from day one**
- Marketplace optimization (metadata, categories, keywords, description)
- **Launch marketing** — Goodreads presence, targeted Reddit engagement, landing page (scope TBD)
- Sales tracking and validation metrics

### Out of Scope (Deferred)

- Print-on-demand editions (Phase 1.5 — evaluate after ebook validation; see §7a)
- Audiobook narration and production (Phase 2)
- Multilingual editions (Phase 3)
- Companion content (reading guides, annotations beyond what's in the edition)
- Paid advertising campaigns

## 3. Validation Hypothesis

**Hypothesis:** A premium illustrated edition of a high-demand public domain text, priced at $4.99–$9.99, can generate meaningful sales despite the availability of free alternatives — differentiated by original illustrations, modernized translation, series design identity, and production quality.

**Revenue model:** This is a catalog-compounding play, not a per-title revenue play. At $7.99 and 35% KDP royalty, a single title nets ~$2.80/sale on Amazon. Non-Amazon channels and direct sales yield significantly more ($5.59 Apple direct, $7.59 via firekeeperbooks.com). Per-title revenue is modest by design — the real economics emerge at 10–20 titles with cross-sell, series brand recognition, and marketplace authority. Wide distribution and direct sales are the economic foundation, not nice-to-haves. The first title proves the pipeline and product-market fit; catalog scale delivers the return. Expect a multi-year value-creation arc, not quick monetization.

**Validation criteria (measured over 60–90 days per title):**

| Metric | Minimum Viable | Strong Signal |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Unit sales (all platforms) | 30–40 (proves willingness to pay at $7.99) | 200+ |
| Average rating | 3.5+ stars | 4.0+ stars |
| Organic rank in category | Top 100 in relevant category | Top 50 |
| Reviews mentioning design/illustrations | Any | 3+ |
| Cross-platform sales (non-Amazon) | Any | 20%+ of total |
| Direct sales (firekeeperbooks.com) | Any | 10%+ of total |

**Threshold rationale:** The original 75-sale minimum assumed meaningful marketplace discovery for a new publisher with zero reviews, zero brand, and no paid ads. That's ambitious. 30–40 sales proves someone will pay for this product; if sales are low but reviews are positive, the diagnosis is distribution (solvable), not product (fundamental). A false negative from insufficient distribution would be the worst outcome — it kills a viable product. Marketing is treated as a learning variable, not a controlled input, during first-title validation.

**Go/no-go gate:** After 2–3 titles published and 60–90 days of data per title, evaluate whether to continue catalog expansion, adjust pricing/positioning, or pivot.

**Kill criteria:** If the first title generates <10 sales in 90 days with no review traction, the market hypothesis is invalidated at the current positioning. Reassess before investing in additional titles.

## 4. First Title: Frankenstein — Mary Shelley

### Why This Title

- **Fiction-first strategy:** Illustrations are the moat, and fiction maximizes that moat. Frankenstein has characters, settings, dramatic scenes — concrete visual anchor points that philosophy lacks.
- #1 on Project Gutenberg all-time downloads. Enduring cultural presence.
- Gothic aesthetic is highly achievable with current AI illustration tools — atmospheric, moody, visually striking.
- Structurally simple: 3 volumes, frame narrative, clear dramatic arc. Short enough to produce quickly, complex enough to showcase the product.
- **No truly illustrated Kindle Frankenstein edition at the quality bar we're targeting.** Existing editions use stock images or minimal-effort covers. Original illustrations = genuine market gap.
- Competitive comparison: the Coulthart Deluxe Edition (print) sets the visual quality bar but isn't available as a well-formatted ebook. The gap is in digital.

**Meditations deferred:** The Stoicism shelf is saturated (Ryan Holiday owns it). Meditations may return as a later catalog title, but fiction-first is the stronger validation play. See §11 for pipeline.

### Source Text

**Edition:** The 1818 text (first edition). Cleanly public domain. Available via Project Gutenberg and Wikisource.

**Why 1818 over 1831:** The 1818 text is rawer, more radical, and closer to Shelley's original voice. It differentiates from the majority of editions (which default to the 1831 revision), aligns with the scholarly trend favoring the first edition, and the rawer voice pairs better with original illustrations. If the 1818 proves too rough in practice, the 1831 is an easy fallback.

### Edition Structure

- Series title page with Firekeeper Books brand identity
- Introduction — who was Mary Shelley, the Villa Diodati origin story, why the 1818 text, how to read it (original content)
- 3 volumes with letters and chapters, each with:
  - Chapter opener with original illustration
  - Full text with clean typography
  - Key interior illustrations at dramatic moments
- Minimal footnotes (only where meaning is genuinely obscured)
- About the series / catalog page at end

**Target illustration count:** 16–20 (cover + chapter openers + key interior scenes). Exceeds KDP's 10-illustration minimum.

## 4a. Second Title: The Odyssey — Homer

### Why This Title

- **Hard external deadline:** Christopher Nolan's Odyssey film releases July 17, 2026. Search demand will spike for months. Timing this edition to the cultural moment is free marketing.
- Naturally episodic — every book is a distinct visual set piece. Cyclops, Sirens, Scylla, Circe, Penelope at the loom. Possibly the single best PD text for an illustrated edition.
- 24 books = 24+ natural illustration breakpoints.
- Visual world (Mediterranean light, mythic scale, monsters and gods) is dramatically different from Frankenstein's gothic aesthetic — proves the series design system works across genres.
- Strong cross-sell potential with future catalog titles (Iliad, Inferno).

### Source Text

**Translation evaluation needed:** Samuel Butler prose (1900) is the default candidate from Gutenberg. Also evaluate: Alexander Pope (verse, 1725), William Cowper (verse, 1791), T.E. Lawrence prose (1932, PD in US). Side-by-side comparison of 2–3 books before committing.

### Edition Structure

- Series title page with Firekeeper Books brand identity
- Introduction — the Odyssey's place in literature, this translation, how to read it, brief note on the 2026 film adaptation (tasteful, not exploitative). Original content.
- 24 books, each with:
  - Book opener with original illustration
  - Full text with clean typography
  - Key interior illustrations at major scenes
- Minimal footnotes
- About the series / catalog page at end

**Target illustration count:** 28–32 (cover + 24 book openers + 3–7 interior scenes).

## 5. Design System

### Series Identity

**Series name: Firekeeper Books.**

**Rationale:** A firekeeper tends the fire so it stays alive for everyone. That's the mission — these texts are the fire, and Firekeeper keeps them burning in a form people can gather around. The name carries warmth, active stewardship, and service. It's personal: Danny and his wife's favorite campfire moment is spreading the embers at the end of the night and watching them glow — light and heat together, consumed for the benefit of all around it.

Works across philosophy, fiction, drama, poetry — not genre-specific. Simple, warm, unpretentious. No competing publisher or imprint uses the name. Domain `firekeeperbooks.com` is available (WHOIS confirmed 2026-03-18). No USPTO trademark conflict found in publishing classes.

**Action item:** Register `firekeeperbooks.com` before the name gets indexed. Verify USPTO Class 016/041 directly at tmsearch.uspto.gov (web scraping couldn't confirm — manual 2-minute check).

**Design aesthetic:** "Scholarly warmth meets functional precision" — Danny's established design language.

- Serif-first typography (transitional or old-style serifs)
- Warm color palette (golds, deep blues, warm grays, cream backgrounds)
- Information density without clutter
- Consistent structural elements across titles (title page layout, chapter openers, margins, ornaments)
- **Per-title accent colors** within the warm series palette:
  - Frankenstein: cold blues, grays, laboratory amber (gothic horror)
  - Odyssey: wine-dark reds, Mediterranean blues, bronze/gold (mythic epic)
  - Future titles define their own accent palette within the series system

### Illustration Style Guide

**Style direction:** To be defined through M-1 spike and M0 prototyping. Key considerations:

- Must be visually coherent across an entire title and across multiple titles in the series
- Should feel timeless, not trend-chasing (avoid hyperrealistic AI art aesthetic)
- Should complement rather than compete with the text
- Line art, woodcut-inspired, or watercolor styles may be more achievable at consistent quality than photorealistic imagery
- Each book's illustration should reflect its thematic content
- **Must work across genres.** The style system must flex from gothic horror (Frankenstein) to mythic epic (Odyssey) to philosophy (future titles) without requiring a redesign. Fiction has stronger visual anchor points (characters, settings, action) which may actually be easier to illustrate consistently. The M-1 spike tests both Frankenstein and Odyssey scenes specifically to validate cross-genre coherence.

**AI illustration tooling:** To be determined through M0 comparative evaluation. The spec does not lock into a single tool. Leading candidates as of March 2026:

- **Midjourney (V7):** Strongest option for stylized, artistic illustration. Style Reference feature enables locking an aesthetic across a series with minimal drift. Mature prompt ecosystem. Weakness: Discord-based workflow can be clunky; character consistency across scenes requires workarounds. $10–30/month.
- **Google Nano Banana 2 (Gemini 3.1 Flash Image):** Launched February 2026. Strong character consistency (up to 5 characters maintained across a workflow), fast iteration, free through Gemini app. Particularly compelling for fiction titles where the same characters appear across multiple illustrations. Weakness: optimized more for photorealism than the timeless artistic styles this project requires. Less mature for tight art direction.
- **DALL-E (via ChatGPT):** Conversational editing loop is low-friction. Good for iterative refinement. Weakness: character identity can drift across longer sequences; less stylistic range than Midjourney.

**M0 comparative evaluation:** Run the same 3–4 illustration prompts (covering both philosophical/abstract and narrative/scenic subjects) through all three tools. Evaluate against:

1. **Timeless aesthetic** — Does it look like book illustration, or does it look like "AI art"? Hyperrealistic/oversaturated AI look is disqualifying.
2. **Cross-illustration coherence** — Can the tool produce 4+ images that feel like they belong in the same book?
3. **Style-lock capability** — Can you establish a visual language and reproduce it reliably across prompts?
4. **Character consistency** (for fiction titles) — Can the same character be recognizably maintained across scenes?
5. **Prompt fidelity** — Does the tool follow specific compositional and stylistic instructions, or does it fight art direction?

A combined workflow is also possible — e.g., Midjourney for style establishment, Nano Banana 2 for character consistency in fiction. M0 should test both single-tool and combined approaches.

**Budget:** $10–30/month (Midjourney) + $0 (Nano Banana 2 via Gemini free tier) + $0 (DALL-E via existing ChatGPT). Total tooling cost remains minimal regardless of which tool wins.

**Critical risk:** Illustration consistency is the single highest technical risk in Phase 1. If none of the evaluated tools can produce a coherent visual series from a style guide and structured prompts, the entire value proposition collapses. M-1 spike provides initial signal; M0 is the hard gate.

## 7. Distribution Strategy

### Platform Priority

| Platform | Priority | Royalty | Notes |
|----------|----------|---------|-------|
| Direct (firekeeperbooks.com) | Highest margin | ~95% via Payhip (~$7.59 net) | Direct upload. Highest per-sale revenue. |
| KDP (Amazon) | Primary volume | 35% for PD content (~$2.80 at $7.99) | Direct upload. No KDP Select. 70% if USCO registration accepted. |
| Kobo Writing Life | Secondary | 70% (~$5.59) | Direct upload. Strong international (190+ countries). |
| Google Play Books | Secondary | 52% (~$4.15) | Direct upload. Smaller market but growing. |
| Apple Books for Authors | Secondary | 70% (~$5.59) | Direct upload (NOT via D2D — see below). |

**Distribution model:** Wide from day one. No KDP Select. **Wide-first, not Amazon-first.** The 35% KDP royalty for public domain content makes Amazon the worst-paying channel. Revenue mix target: 50% KDP / 15% direct / 15% Kobo / 10% Apple / 10% Google. Blended per-sale: ~$4.02 (35% KDP) or ~$5.30 (if 70% KDP achieved).

**KDP 35% PD restriction:** KDP restricts public domain content to 35% royalty. "The 70% royalty option is for in-copyright works only." Escape hatch: substantial original content (16+ illustrations, original introduction, editorial adaptation) may qualify as derivative work eligible for 70%. Requires USCO copyright registration ($65, 3–11 months processing) and selecting "I own the copyright" at upload. **Title #1 launches at 35%. 70% is a Title #2 play** once USCO registration is in place. See `title-01-frankenstein/research/` for detailed findings.

**KDP Select rationale:** KDP Select's promotional tools would juice numbers in ways that contaminate the validation signal. KU reads count as "sales" but represent fundamentally different buyer behavior. Going wide produces cleaner data, avoids 90-day exclusivity lock-in, and tests multi-platform viability immediately.

**D2D prohibition:** Draft2Digital prohibits public domain content. Apple Books must be published directly via Apple Books for Authors. This actually improves per-sale economics (no 10% aggregator cut: $5.59 direct vs. $5.03 via D2D).

### Pricing Strategy

**Phase 1 price:** $7.99

**Rationale (revised from $5.99):** The 35% KDP royalty on PD content changes the math. At $5.99/35%, Amazon net is ~$2.10/sale — uncomfortably thin. $7.99 yields ~$2.80 on Amazon and $5.59+ on non-Amazon platforms. The competitive landscape is bimodal: free/$0.99 minimal-effort editions, and $9.99+ premium editions. $7.99 positions clearly in the premium tier while remaining an impulse buy.

**Pricing experiments (after first title data):** Test $9.99 on the Odyssey (film-driven demand may support higher pricing). $9.99 is the ceiling — that's Penguin Classics territory and requires brand credibility the series hasn't earned yet.

### Marketplace Optimization

- **Categories:** Title-specific. Frankenstein: Horror > Gothic, Literature & Fiction > Classics, Science Fiction > Classic. Odyssey: Literature & Fiction > Mythology, Classic Literature. Biographies of Philosophers has thinner competition for philosophy titles.
- **Keywords:** Research top-performing keywords per title on KDP. For Odyssey, include film-related search terms.
- **Description:** Emphasize original illustrations, design quality, and series identity. Per KDP PD requirements: include "(Illustrated)" in title field, bullet-point differentiation summary. Lead with the visual experience.
- **A+ Content (KDP):** If eligible, use enhanced product page with illustration samples

### Launch Marketing

Revisit scope during M1, but baseline activities for each title launch:

- Goodreads author/series presence
- Targeted posts in relevant Reddit communities (r/stoicism, r/philosophy, r/ebooks)
- Simple landing page / series website
- Additional channels TBD based on first-title learnings

## 7a. Print-on-Demand (Phase 1.5 — Evaluate After Ebook Validation)

POD editions are not in Phase 1 scope but are a natural extension once the ebook pipeline is proven. Evaluate after M3 validation data is in, based on learned experience.

### Opportunity

- Illustrated editions are natural display objects — physical books have a gifting and shelf-presence dimension that ebooks lack
- Higher price ceiling ($14.99–24.99 for illustrated paperbacks vs $5.99 ebook)
- Cross-format presence strengthens marketplace search visibility and authority
- Zero inventory risk — print on demand, Amazon/Ingram handles fulfillment

### Key Considerations

- **Ebook layout ≠ print layout.** Reformatting is real work: page dimensions, margins, bleed, spine width calculation, print-specific typography. Not a one-click export.
- **Illustration resolution.** Print requires 300 DPI; ebook is 72 DPI. AI illustration generation should target 300 DPI from the start to keep the POD path open without regeneration.
- **Cover format.** Print needs a wrap-around cover with spine. Ebook cover is front-only.
- **Proof copies.** QA requires ordering physical proofs ($5–15 + shipping + review time per title).
- **Print royalties are lower.** KDP Print: list price minus printing cost minus 40% Amazon cut. Margins are thinner than ebook.

### Platform Options

- **KDP Print (Amazon POD):** Easiest path — same dashboard as ebook. But Amazon's own POD quality is degrading (see [[erhardt-amazon-pod-enshittification]]). Matte finishes, grainy covers, poor typesetting. Your beautifully designed interior may be let down by the print production.
- **IngramSpark:** Higher print quality, wider distribution (libraries, bookstores, international). More complex setup, $49/title fee. Better fit for a premium illustrated product.

### Decision Framework (evaluate at M3)

- Did ebook reviews or reader feedback indicate demand for physical copies?
- Is the design system mature enough that print reformatting is bounded work?
- Does IngramSpark quality justify the per-title fee and setup complexity?
- What's the actual illustration DPI from the AI tools — can they support 300 DPI without regeneration?

**Action for now:** Generate all illustrations at 300 DPI from M-1 onward to keep the POD path open. No other POD work until post-M3 evaluation.

## 9. Entity & Legal Considerations

### Michigan Invention & Moonlighting Law

Michigan does not have an employee invention protection statute. The nine states with explicit statutory protections are: California, Delaware, Illinois, Kansas, Minnesota, New Jersey, North Carolina, Washington, and New York. Michigan is not among them.

In Michigan, employee IP rights default to common law (shop right doctrine, hired-to-invent, scope of employment) and whatever the employment agreement says. None of these doctrines apply to Wisdom Library — publishing illustrated ebooks of ancient philosophy is entirely unrelated to DDI/DNS security, uses no Infoblox resources/time/equipment/trade secrets. The remaining risk is contractual — addressed by the PIIA Exhibit B carve-out (see §8).

### LLC Formation

Deferred to post-M3 validation. No meaningful liability exposure from ebook publishing, and no reason to spend on entity formation before the product validates. Publish under Danny's name for the first 2–3 titles. If catalog expansion proceeds, form Michigan LLC (~$50 filing fee) so the publisher name on KDP matches the LLC.

### Tax

Self-employment income. Consult accountant for quarterly estimated tax obligations once revenue starts. At Phase 1 volumes, likely negligible, but structure should be correct from the start.

### AI Art Copyright

Legally ambiguous. AI-generated illustrations may not be copyrightable under current US Copyright Office guidance, which means limited IP protection for the visual identity. The brand, series consistency, and curation are the moat, not individual image copyrights.

### KDP AI Disclosure

Required during title setup. Confidential — not shown to customers. Not a friction point.

### Selection Criteria

When choosing the next title to produce, evaluate against:

1. **Visual potential** — Does the text provide concrete, illustratable moments? Fiction/drama generally score higher than abstract philosophy.
2. **Audience breadth** — How large is the existing readership? (Goodreads ratings, Gutenberg downloads as proxies)
3. **Structural fit** — Does the text have natural illustration breakpoints (chapters, cantos, books)?
4. **Genre balance** — Alternate genres to keep the catalog diverse. Don't let philosophy dominate just because it started there.
5. **Production efficiency** — Shorter texts are faster to produce; sequence a mix of long and short.
6. **Cross-sell potential** — Does this title pair with or draw traffic from existing catalog entries?

**Key tension:** Philosophy texts initiated this project, but fiction generally scores higher on visual potential and audience breadth. The Odyssey or Inferno could arguably be a stronger first title than Meditations — Meditations is philosophically rich but visually abstract (illustrating themes, not scenes), while the Odyssey gives you Polyphemus, Circe, the wine-dark sea. The Stoic market data still favors Meditations for validation, but this is worth revisiting at M0 if the design prototype reveals that philosophy is harder to illustrate compellingly.

**Note:** Danny's personal library may surface additional candidates. Copyright status must be verified per-title (pre-1928 US publication or life+70 in relevant jurisdictions).
