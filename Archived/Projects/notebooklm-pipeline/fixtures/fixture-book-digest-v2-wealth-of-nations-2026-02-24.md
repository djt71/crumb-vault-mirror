## Core Thesis
Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* argues that the true wealth of a nation is not its accumulated hoard of gold and silver, but the total annual produce of its land and labour. Smith contends that this wealth is maximized through the division of labour, which enhances productivity, and the accumulation of capital, which puts productive labour to work. He fundamentally critiques the "mercantile system" of his time—which favored protectionism, monopolies, and the hoarding of specie—and advocates for a "system of natural liberty". In this framework, individuals pursuing their own self-interest, regulated by market competition, unintentionally promote the general welfare of society more effectively than government planning could achieve.

## Key Arguments
**The Division of Labour drives productivity.**
Smith argues that the greatest improvements in the productive powers of labour are the effects of the division of labour. He attributes this increase to three circumstances: the increase of dexterity in every particular workman, the saving of time lost in passing from one species of work to another, and the invention of machinery which facilitates labour. He illustrates this with the trade of a pin-maker, where ten men, by performing distinct operations, could produce upwards of forty-eight thousand pins a day, whereas one man working alone could scarce make twenty.

**Labour is the real measure of value.**
While commodities have a nominal price in money, their real value is determined by the "toil and trouble" of acquiring them. Smith asserts that labour was the "first price, the original purchase money that was paid for all things". Labour is the universal standard by which the value of all commodities can be compared across all times and places, whereas the value of money fluctuates.

**Capital accumulation determines the quantity of industry.**
Smith distinguishes between productive labour (which adds value to a subject, like manufacturing) and unproductive labour (services which perish in the instant, like menial servants or the sovereign). He argues that capital is increased by parsimony (saving), which funds productive labour, and is diminished by prodigality and misconduct. Therefore, the proportion of industrious people to idle people in a nation depends on the proportion of capital to revenue.

**The Mercantile System is counterproductive.**
Smith devotes significant space to dismantling the idea that money is wealth and that nations must secure a "favorable balance of trade". He argues that restraints on imports (high duties, prohibitions) and encouragements to exports (bounties) misallocate capital from more advantageous to less advantageous channels. He states that consumption is the sole end of production, and the mercantile system wrongly sacrifices the interest of the consumer to that of the producer.

**The Sovereign has limited duties.**
Under the system of natural liberty, the state is discharged from the duty of superintending the industry of private people. The sovereign has only three duties: protecting society from violence and invasion (defense), protecting every member from injustice or oppression (justice), and erecting and maintaining certain public works and institutions (such as education and roads) which private individuals cannot profitably maintain.

## Key Concepts & Frameworks
*   **Division of Labour** — The separation of a trade or task into distinct operations to increase efficiency. Smith uses this to explain the massive increase in productivity in civilized societies, citing the pin factory where operations are split into eighteen distinct parts.
*   **Invisible Hand** — A metaphor describing how an individual, intending only his own security and gain, is led by an invisible mechanism to promote an end (the public good) which was no part of his intention. This occurs most effectively when trade is free.
*   **Natural Price vs. Market Price** — The **Natural Price** is the price sufficient to pay the rent, wages, and profits employed in bringing a commodity to market. The **Market Price** is the actual price at which goods are sold, regulated by the proportion between the quantity brought to market and the effectual demand. Market prices gravitate toward the natural price.
*   **Productive vs. Unproductive Labour** — **Productive labour** adds to the value of the subject upon which it is bestowed and fixes itself in a vendible commodity (e.g., manufacturers). **Unproductive labour** does not add value and perishes in the instant of performance (e.g., menial servants, the sovereign, players, buffoons).
*   **Fixed vs. Circulating Capital** — **Fixed Capital** yields a revenue or profit without changing masters (e.g., machines, buildings, improvements of land). **Circulating Capital** yields a revenue only by circulating or changing masters (e.g., money, provisions, goods for sale).
*   **The Four Maxims of Taxation** — A framework for fair taxation: 1) Equality (contributing in proportion to revenue), 2) Certainty (not arbitrary), 3) Convenience (payment time and manner), and 4) Economy (taking out as little as possible over what enters the treasury).

## Checklists & Procedures
**The Four Maxims of Taxation**
1.  **Equality:** Subjects should contribute towards the support of the government as nearly as possible in proportion to their respective abilities (revenue).
2.  **Certainty:** The tax which each individual is bound to pay ought to be certain and not arbitrary. The time, manner, and quantity should be clear.
3.  **Convenience:** Every tax ought to be levied at the time, or in the manner, most likely to be convenient for the contributor.
4.  **Economy:** Every tax ought to be so contrived as to take out and keep out of the pockets of the people as little as possible over and above what it brings into the public treasury.

**Natural Progress of Opulence**
Smith outlines a natural order for the employment of capital in a growing society:
1.  **Agriculture:** Capital is first directed to the cultivation of land.
2.  **Manufactures:** Capital is next directed to manufactures.
3.  **Foreign Commerce:** Finally, capital is directed to foreign commerce.

## Takeaways & Applications
*   **Policymakers and Governments:** Should avoid attempting to direct the industry of private people, as they lack the necessary local knowledge and interest. Instead of imposing tariffs and bounties, they should focus on defense, justice, and infrastructure.
*   **Tax Authorities:** Should design tax systems that adhere to the four maxims: equality, certainty, convenience, and economy. Taxes on rent, profit, and wages should be carefully considered to avoid discouraging industry.
*   **Business Owners:** Efficiency and productivity can be dramatically increased by implementing the division of labour within operations.
*   **Investors/Capitalists:** Capital naturally seeks the most advantageous employment (profit). However, high profits can destroy parsimony (frugality), which is necessary for capital accumulation.
*   **Consumers:** Understanding that monopolies and trade restrictions (like the corn laws or colonial trade monopolies) generally serve the interest of the producer at the expense of the consumer.

## Uncertain / Needs Verification
*   Smith's calculations regarding the prices of wheat in ancient times are based on historical records available to him (like Bishop Fleetwood's *Chronicon Preciosum*) and may differ from modern economic historical analysis.
*   Smith's assertion that the value of silver was falling or rising at specific periods is a matter of historical economic debate.
*   His critique of the "unproductive" nature of service professions (physicians, lawyers, artists) is a specific economic definition that differs from modern conceptions of value in the service economy.

## Connections
*   **Classical Economics:** This work is the foundational text for the classical school, influencing Ricardo, Malthus, and Mill.
*   **Modern Free Market Economics:** The concept of the "invisible hand" and the critique of protectionism remain central to modern arguments for free trade and deregulation.
*   **Political Science:** Smith's discussion of the three duties of the sovereign (defense, justice, public works) connects to theories of limited government and the night-watchman state.
*   **Colonial History:** The analysis of the American colonies provides primary insight into the economic tensions preceding the American Revolution.
