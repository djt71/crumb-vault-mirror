## Core Thesis

John Rawls presents "justice as fairness," a comprehensive theory of justice meant to provide a systematic alternative to utilitarianism. He argues that the most reasonable principles of justice for the basic structure of society are those that free and equal rational persons would agree to in a hypothetical "original position" of equality, behind a "veil of ignorance" that prevents them from knowing their specific place in society or natural assets. The resulting theory prioritizes equal basic liberties and mandates that social and economic inequalities be arranged to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged members of society.

## Key Arguments

- **Justice as Fairness:** The guiding idea is that the principles of justice are the object of an original agreement made under conditions that are fair to all parties. This procedure ensures that the outcome is just, utilizing the concept of pure procedural justice.
- **The Original Position and Veil of Ignorance:** To insure impartiality, principles are chosen in a hypothetical situation where parties do not know their class position, natural talents, or specific conception of the good. This forces them to choose principles that are fair to everyone, regardless of where they end up in the social hierarchy.
- **Rejection of Utilitarianism:** Rational parties in the original position would reject the principle of utility because it could justify sacrificing the rights and interests of some for the greater aggregate good. They would not risk their own basic liberties and well-being on the chance of a higher total social utility.
- **The Two Principles of Justice:** The parties would essentially choose two principles:
    1. **Equal Liberty:** Each person has an equal right to the most extensive scheme of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar scheme for all.
    2. **Social and Economic Inequality:** Inequalities must satisfy two conditions: they must be attached to offices open to all under fair equality of opportunity, and they must be to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged members of society (the Difference Principle).
- **The Priority of Liberty:** The first principle (liberty) is lexically prior to the second (social/economic); this means basic liberties cannot be traded for greater economic advantages, but can only be restricted for the sake of liberty itself.
- **The Maximin Strategy:** The choice of the two principles is analogous to the "maximin" rule for choice under uncertainty, where one chooses the alternative with the best worst-case outcome, protecting oneself against the risk of an intolerable life.
- **Stability and Congruence:** A well-ordered society governed by these principles will be stable because it generates its own support; the sense of justice acquired by citizens is congruent with their own good, particularly the primary good of self-respect.

## Key Concepts

- **Original Position** — A hypothetical initial status quo of equality where rational parties choose the principles of justice, ensuring fairness in the agreement.
- **Veil of Ignorance** — The condition in the original position where parties are deprived of knowledge about their particular circumstances (class, talent, psychology) to prevent bias in the selection of principles.
- **Difference Principle** — The rule that social and economic inequalities are just only if they result in compensating benefits for everyone, and specifically maximize the expectations of the least advantaged group.
- **Basic Structure** — The primary subject of justice; the way major social institutions (constitution, economy, family) distribute fundamental rights and duties and determine the division of social advantages.
- **Primary Goods** — Things that every rational man is presumed to want, such as rights, liberties, opportunities, income, wealth, and self-respect.
- **Reflective Equilibrium** — A state of coherence reached when our principles of justice match our considered judgments (and vice versa) after due reflection and adjustment.
- **Lexical Order** — A ranking of principles where the first must be fully satisfied before the second comes into play; specifically, the priority of liberty over social and economic welfare.
- **Well-Ordered Society** — A society effectively regulated by a public conception of justice, where everyone accepts the same principles and institutions satisfy them.

## Notable Quotes

> "Justice is the first virtue of social institutions, as truth is of systems of thought. A theory however elegant and economical must be rejected or revised if it is untrue; likewise laws and institutions no matter how efficient and well-arranged must be reformed or abolished if they are unjust."

> "Each person possesses an inviolability founded on justice that even the welfare of society as a whole cannot override. For this reason justice denies that the loss of freedom for some is made right by a greater good shared by others."

> "The principles of justice are chosen behind a veil of ignorance. This ensures that no one is advantaged or disadvantaged in the choice of principles by the outcome of natural chance or the contingency of social circumstances."

> "Utilitarianism does not take seriously the distinction between persons."

> "Social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are both (a) to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged, consistent with the just savings principle, and (b) attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity."

## Takeaways & Applications

- **Constitutional Design:** The theory provides a framework for a four-stage sequence of creating a just society: choosing principles, framing a constitution, enacting legislation, and applying rules to particular cases.
- **Distributive Justice:** It argues against meritocracy and pure market outcomes, suggesting that economic systems (whether capitalist or socialist) must define property rights and taxes to benefit the worst-off.
- **Civil Disobedience:** The theory offers a justification for civil disobedience in a nearly just democracy as a way to address the majority's sense of justice when basic liberties are violated.
- **Moral Methodology:** The method of "reflective equilibrium" suggests we should balance our theoretical principles against our strongest intuitive convictions about justice.

## Uncertain / Needs Verification

- **Psychological Assumptions:** The argument relies on specific assumptions about human psychology (e.g., lack of envy in the original position, the Aristotelian Principle) which Rawls claims are "general facts" but are essentially theoretical postulates within the book.
- **Economic Theory:** The text assumes certain economic mechanisms (like the functioning of competitive markets and the effectiveness of taxation branches) will work as theoretically described to achieve justice.
- **Detachment from History:** The "original position" is purely hypothetical and ahistorical; some critics argue it abstracts too much from the specific cultural and historical contexts that shape actual conceptions of justice.

## Connections

- **Social Contract Tradition:** Explicitly builds upon and generalizes the theories of **Locke**, **Rousseau**, and **Kant**, taking the social contract to a higher level of abstraction.
- **Utilitarianism:** Written largely as a reaction against and alternative to the classical utilitarianism of **Bentham**, **Sidgwick**, and **Mill**.
- **Kantian Ethics:** The theory is given a "Kantian interpretation," where acting on principles chosen in the original position is seen as an expression of our nature as free, equal, and rational noumenal selves.
- **Economics and Game Theory:** Incorporates concepts like **Pareto optimality** (efficiency), the **maximin rule**, and the **prisoner's dilemma** (isolation/assurance problems).
