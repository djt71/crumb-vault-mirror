# Chapter 1: Of the Division of Labour

## Summary
This chapter introduces the division of labour as the primary engine for improving the productive powers of labour and refining the skill, dexterity, and judgment with which it is applied. Building on the Introduction's premise that a nation's wealth derives from its annual labour, Smith argues that the efficiency of this labour determines the abundance of supply. While the Introduction sets the stage by defining the annual labour as the fund of a nation, Chapter 1 provides the specific mechanism—specialization—that allows this fund to grow effectively. It establishes the foundational economic principle that productivity is not just about the number of laborers, but how their work is organized.

The arguments presented here set the stage for the subsequent chapters, particularly Chapter 2, which explores the human propensity to truck, barter, and exchange as the root cause of this division, and Chapter 3, which discusses how the extent of the market limits this division. Smith's detailed analysis of the pin factory illustrates how subdividing tasks leads to exponential increases in output, a concept he then applies to the broader economy. This chapter contributes to the book's overall thesis by demonstrating that the division of labour is the fundamental cause of the "universal opulence" found in civilized societies, allowing even the lowest ranks of people to enjoy a standard of living superior to that of many African kings.

## Key Points
**The Division of Labour Increases Productive Powers**
Smith claims that the greatest improvements in the productive powers of labour are the effects of the division of labour. He supports this with the example of a pin-maker. While a single workman could scarcely make one pin a day, the trade is divided into about eighteen distinct operations, such as drawing the wire, straightening it, and cutting it. Smith observes that ten men, by performing distinct operations, could produce upwards of forty-eight thousand pins in a day, a feat impossible if they worked independently.

**Three Circumstances Cause the Increase in Quantity of Work**
The substantial increase in work performed by the same number of people is attributed to three factors: the increase of dexterity in every particular workman, the saving of time usually lost in passing from one species of work to another, and the invention of machines. By reducing a man's business to a single operation, his dexterity is necessarily increased. Smith contrasts a common smith, who might make 200 or 300 bad nails a day, with a specialized nailer who can make upwards of 2,300, illustrating the impact of specialized dexterity.

**Saving Time and Avoiding "Sauntering"**
Smith argues that significant time is lost when a workman shifts from one task to another, especially if it requires moving to a different place or using different tools. He notes that a country weaver who also farms loses time moving between the loom and the field. Furthermore, he claims that men naturally "saunter" a little when changing tasks, rendering them "slothful and lazy" and incapable of vigorous application, which considerably reduces the quantity of work they can perform.

**Invention of Machinery**
The invention of machines that facilitate labour is largely due to the division of labour itself. Smith posits that men are more likely to discover easier methods of attaining an object when their whole attention is directed towards that single object. He cites the example of a boy employed to open and shut the communication between the boiler and cylinder of a fire engine (steam engine), who used a string to automate the process so he could play with his companions, demonstrating how specialized focus leads to innovation.

**Universal Opulence and Interdependence**
The division of labour results in a "universal opulence" that extends to the lowest ranks of the people. Smith explains that every workman has a large quantity of his own work to exchange for the goods of others, creating a general plenty. He uses the example of a day-labourer's woollen coat to show that even a simple product is the result of the joint labour of a multitude of workmen—from the shepherd and sorter to the dyer and weaver—along with merchants, carriers, and ship-builders. This highlights the deep interdependence and cooperation required to supply even the most basic needs in a civilized country.

## Notable Quotes
> "The greatest improvements in the productive powers of labour, and the greater part of the skill, dexterity, and judgment, with which it is anywhere directed, or applied, seem to have been the effects of the division of labour."

> "Each person, therefore, making a tenth part of forty-eight thousand pins, might be considered as making four thousand eight hundred pins in a day. But if they had all wrought separately and independently, and without any of them having been educated to this peculiar business, they certainly could not each of them have made twenty, perhaps not one pin in a day..."

> "It is the great multiplication of the productions of all the different arts, in consequence of the division of labour, which occasions, in a well-governed society, that universal opulence which extends itself to the lowest ranks of the people."
