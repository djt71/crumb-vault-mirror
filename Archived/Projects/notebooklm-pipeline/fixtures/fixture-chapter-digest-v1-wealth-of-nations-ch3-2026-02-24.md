## Chapter 3: That the Division of Labour is Limited by the Extent of the Market

### Summary
This chapter qualifies the arguments of the previous chapter by introducing a critical constraint: the extent of the market. Smith argues that because the division of labour is derived from the power of exchanging, the degree of specialization is necessarily limited by the ability to exchange, or the size of the market. This concept explains why urbanization and access to transportation routes (specifically water carriage) are crucial for economic development.

It connects to the previous discussion on the origins of specialized labour and sets the stage for the next chapter on money, which facilitates the market's operation. Smith uses this principle to explain historical economic geography, noting why civilization and improvements in art and industry first thrived in coastal regions and along navigable rivers like the Nile and Ganges, while inland areas remained barbarous.

### Key Points
**Market Size and Specialization**
When the market is very small, individuals have no encouragement to dedicate themselves entirely to one employment because they cannot exchange their surplus produce for the parts of others' labour they need. In lonely houses or small villages, like those in the Highlands of Scotland, families must perform many different tasks (butcher, baker, brewer) themselves, as the market is too narrow to support specialized tradesmen like a nailer, who would produce far more nails in a day than a small market could consume in a year.

**Advantages of Water Carriage**
Water carriage opens a more extensive market to every sort of industry than land carriage alone can afford. Smith illustrates this by comparing a broad-wheeled waggon attended by two men and eight horses, carrying four tons between London and Edinburgh in six weeks, to a ship navigated by six or eight men carrying two hundred tons in the same time. The ship accomplishes what would require fifty waggons, a hundred men, and four hundred horses. Without water carriage, commerce between distant places would be impossible due to the expense, and industry would be discouraged.

**Historical Progress and Geography**
Because water carriage opens the whole world as a market, the first improvements in art and industry naturally occur in coastal areas and along navigable rivers. Improvements extend much later to inland parts of the country, which can only trade with their immediate neighbours. Smith cites ancient Egypt (the Nile), Bengal (the Ganges), and eastern China as early civilizations that prospered due to extensive inland navigation. Conversely, he notes that inland parts of Africa and Tartary (Asia) have remained in a barbarous state because they lack the "great inlets" of navigable rivers to facilitate commerce.

### Notable Quotes
> "As it is the power of exchanging that gives occasion to the division of labour, so the extent of this division must always be limited by the extent of that power, or, in other words, by the extent of the market. When the market is very small, no person can have any encouragement to dedicate himself entirely to one employment, for want of the power to exchange all that surplus part of the produce of his own labour, which is over and above his own consumption, for such parts of the produce of other men's labour as he has occasion for."

> "Six or eight men, therefore, by the help of water-carriage, can carry and bring back, in the same time, the same quantity of goods between London and Edinburgh as fifty broad-wheeled waggons, attended by a hundred men, and drawn by four hundred horses."

> "Since such, therefore, are the advantages of water-carriage, it is natural that the first improvements of art and industry should be made where this conveniency opens the whole world for a market to the produce of every sort of labour, and that they should always be much later in extending themselves into the inland parts of the country."
