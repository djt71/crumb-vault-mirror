# The Confessions of St. Augustine by Saint Augustine

### Book I

**Summary**
Augustine begins with an invocation of praise to God, establishing the core premise of his work: human beings are inherently restless until they find peace in their Creator. He reflects on his infancy and boyhood, periods he cannot directly remember but infers through observation of other children and the testimonies of his parents. He highlights that even infants exhibit self-will, jealousy, and sin, disproving the notion of inherent human innocence. 

As he transitions into boyhood, Augustine recounts his early education. He developed a deep hatred for studying Greek and mathematics, preferring instead to play games and read the Latin poetic fictions of Virgil, such as the story of Aeneas and Dido. He critiques the societal values that praised his rhetorical eloquence in recounting pagan myths while ignoring the moral decay of his soul. He also notes his mother Monnica’s early piety, contrasting it with his father's worldly ambitions for him.

**Key Points**
*   **Original Sin and Infant Depravity:** Humans are born with a propensity for sin, evidenced by the selfishness, tantrums, and jealousy seen even in infants.
*   **Misplaced Educational Values:** Society prioritizes rhetorical skill and the pursuit of wealth/status over moral and spiritual development.
*   **The Restless Heart:** The fundamental human condition is one of seeking, which can only be truly satisfied by returning to God.

**Notable Quotes**
> "Thou awakest us to delight in Thy praise; for Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee." (p. 3)

> "The weakness then of infant limbs, not its will, is its innocence. Myself have seen and known even a baby envious; it could not speak, yet it turned pale and looked bitterly on its foster-brother." (p. 7)

### Book II

**Summary**
Augustine examines his adolescence, particularly his sixteenth year, during which he was forced to pause his studies due to his family's lack of funds. Idleness led him into a "cauldron of unholy loves" and deep carnal corruption. He reflects on his father's joy at seeing his physical maturation, which only anticipated worldly descendants, while his mother secretly worried for his spiritual state but did not yet press him to marry, fearing it would hinder his education. 

The focal point of this book is the infamous theft of the pears. Augustine analyzes a night when he and a group of friends stole pears from a neighbor's tree—not because they were hungry or because the pears were good (they threw them to the pigs), but simply for the thrill of doing something forbidden. He concludes that the sin was driven entirely by the desire to break the law and by the toxic camaraderie of his peer group.

**Key Points**
*   **Sin for Sin's Sake:** Human depravity can be so profound that individuals will commit evil acts not for a tangible reward, but simply for the love of the transgression itself.
*   **The Danger of Bad Company:** The influence of peers ("O friendship too unfriendly!") amplifies sinful desires; Augustine notes he would not have stolen the pears alone.
*   **Misguided Parental Ambition:** His parents' singular focus on his secular education and future worldly success blinded them to his moral deterioration.

**Notable Quotes**
> "I loved to perish, I loved mine own fault, not that for which I was faulty, but my fault itself. Foul soul, falling from Thy firmament to utter destruction..." (p. 19)

> "What then did wretched I so love in thee, thou theft of mine, thou deed of darkness, in that sixteenth year of my age? Lovely thou wert not, because thou wert theft." (p. 19-20)

### Book III

**Summary**
Moving to Carthage to continue his education, Augustine is immediately swept up in a culture of theatrical shows and illicit romances. He describes his love of tragedy and theater as a "miserable madness," where he sought out artificial grief to stimulate his emotions. During this period, he reads Cicero's *Hortensius*, which ignites in him a profound, burning desire for eternal wisdom and pulls him away from purely superficial pursuits. 

However, his search for truth takes a disastrous turn when he joins the Manichees, a dualistic sect that offered a hyper-rational, materialistic explanation of the universe and mocked the Catholic faith. Augustine is captivated by their promises of hidden knowledge. Meanwhile, his mother Monnica weeps bitterly for his soul. She receives a comforting dream showing Augustine standing on the same "wooden rule" as her, and a bishop advises her that "the son of these tears should [not] perish."

**Key Points**
*   **The Awakening of Philosophical Desire:** Cicero's *Hortensius* serves as a crucial turning point, shifting Augustine's focus from mere rhetorical style to the pursuit of objective truth.
*   **The Trap of Manichaeism:** Augustine falls for Manichaeism because it flatters his pride, offers a convenient scapegoat for sin (blaming an external "evil" nature), and appeals to his inability to conceive of a non-material God.
*   **Maternal Intercession:** Monnica's relentless weeping and praying for her son act as a spiritual anchor, foreshadowing his eventual return to the Church.

**Notable Quotes**
> "To Carthage I came, where there sang all around me in my ears a cauldron of unholy loves." (p. 22)

> "Go thy ways and God bless thee, for it is not possible that the son of these tears should perish." (p. 32)

### Book IV

**Summary**
Spanning the years from nineteen to twenty-eight, this book covers Augustine's career as a teacher of rhetoric in Thagaste and Carthage. He takes a concubine and becomes deeply interested in astrology, rejecting the warnings of wise men that it is a fraudulent science. This period is marked by a profound personal tragedy: the sudden death of a very close, unnamed friend. 

The loss devastates Augustine, making his hometown unbearable and driving him back to Carthage. He analyzes the nature of his grief, realizing that his misery stemmed from loving a mortal being as if he were immortal. Trying to find solace in worldly beauty, he writes a philosophical treatise, *On the Fair and Fit*, dedicating it to a Roman orator he idolizes. He also reads Aristotle's *Categories*, attempting to apply its logic to God, which only deepens his materialist misconceptions of the Divine.

**Key Points**
*   **The Nature of Earthly Grief:** Loving mutable, created things as ultimate ends inevitably leads to despair when those things pass away. True peace requires loving friends "in God."
*   **Intellectual Pride as a Hindrance:** Augustine's sharp intellect and independent study of Aristotle become stumbling blocks, leading him to incorrectly conceptualize God as a material substance subject to logical categories.
*   **Rejection of Astrology:** Though he dabbles in it, conversations with intellectuals plant the seeds of doubt regarding astrological determinism, moving him toward a belief in free will and divine providence.

**Notable Quotes**
> "And what am I to myself without Thee, but a guide to mine own downfall?" (p. 33)

> "For whithersoever the soul of man turns itself, unless toward Thee, it is riveted upon sorrows, yea though it is riveted on things beautiful." (p. 38)

### Book V

**Summary**
Augustine reaches his twenty-ninth year and eagerly anticipates the arrival of Faustus, a highly praised Manichaean bishop who is supposed to resolve all of Augustine’s doubts about the sect's cosmology and theology. Upon meeting Faustus, Augustine finds him highly eloquent but intellectually hollow; Faustus cannot answer Augustine's scientific and philosophical questions. This shatters Augustine's faith in Manichaeism, though he does not leave the sect entirely.

Seeking better students, Augustine moves to Rome—deceiving his mother to slip away—but finds the students there dishonest. He secures a rhetoric professorship in Milan, where he meets the Catholic Bishop Ambrose. Drawn initially by Ambrose’s renowned rhetorical skill, Augustine gradually begins to absorb the substance of his sermons. Ambrose’s allegorical interpretations of the Old Testament dissolve Augustine's previous Manichaean objections, leaving him a Catechumen in the Catholic Church, though still suspending full belief.

**Key Points**
*   **Disillusionment with Manichaeism:** The inability of Faustus to reconcile Manichaean doctrine with scientific reality breaks the intellectual spell the sect had on Augustine.
*   **The Role of Eloquence:** Augustine recognizes that beautiful words do not guarantee truth, nor do plain words indicate falsehood; yet, Ambrose's eloquence becomes the vehicle through which God delivers truth to him.
*   **Allegorical Exegesis:** Ambrose introduces Augustine to the spiritual/allegorical reading of the Old Testament, removing the literalist stumbling blocks that the Manichees had exploited.

**Notable Quotes**
> "For fairer is the modesty of a candid mind, than the knowledge of those things which I desired; and such I found him, in all the more difficult and subtile questions." (p. 50)

> "To him was I unknowing led by Thee, that by him I might knowingly be led to Thee." (p. 55)

### Book VI

**Summary**
Monnica arrives in Milan and finds Augustine no longer a Manichee, but not yet a Catholic. She confidently predicts she will see him fully converted before she dies. Augustine is now thirty, highly ambitious, and plagued by anxiety over his career, wealth, and marriage prospects. He compares his own miserable, anxiety-ridden state to the carefree happiness of a drunken beggar he passes on the street. 

The book deeply profiles two of Augustine’s closest friends, Alypius and Nebridius. Alypius is famously dragged into an addiction to gladiatorial games after initially despising them, illustrating the overpowering nature of habit and crowd psychology. Augustine struggles fiercely with his own sexual desires. Though he and his friends contemplate a communal philosophical life, the plan collapses because they cannot agree on the issue of marriage and celibacy. Under pressure from his mother, Augustine gets engaged to a young girl, resulting in the painful dismissal of his long-time concubine, which breaks his heart and quickly leads him to take another lover.

**Key Points**
*   **Worldly Ambition vs. True Joy:** The pursuit of wealth, honor, and marriage brings Augustine only paralyzing anxiety, contrasting sharply with the simple (if misguided) joy of the beggar.
*   **The Power of Habit and Peer Pressure:** Alypius’s fall into the bloodlust of the arena demonstrates how easily the soul is corrupted when it relies on its own strength rather than God's.
*   **The Bondage of Lust:** Augustine's inability to remain celibate even after his concubine leaves him reveals the deep, addictive chaining of his will to sexual pleasure.

**Notable Quotes**
> "I was healthfully distracted and dying, to live; knowing what evil thing I was, and not knowing what good thing I was shortly to become." (p. 95)

> "Woe is me! Lord, have pity on me. My evil sorrows strive with my good joys; and on which side is the victory, I know not." (p. 132) *(Note: This quote structurally belongs to the themes of inner conflict present from Book VI onward, though explicitly vocalized later).*

### Book VII

**Summary**
Augustine finally resolves the intellectual obstacles keeping him from God. He struggles to conceptualize a God who is infinite and omnipresent but not a physical substance. Through a friend, he obtains the books of the Platonists (Neoplatonism), which teach him to seek truth inwardly and to understand that God is a spiritual, immaterial reality. 

This philosophical breakthrough also solves the problem of evil for him: he realizes that evil is not an independent substance or a competing cosmic power (as the Manichees claimed), but rather a *privation* or corruption of goodness. All things that exist are good because they are created by God, but they are subject to corruption. However, while the Platonists help him understand the eternal Word, they completely lack the concept of the "Word made flesh." Recognizing his own pride, Augustine turns to the Epistles of St. Paul, finding the grace, humility, and Mediator (Christ) that philosophy lacked.

**Key Points**
*   **Immateriality of God:** The Platonists help Augustine break free from materialism, allowing him to perceive God as an infinite, unchangeable, spiritual light.
*   **The Privation Theory of Evil:** Evil is not a substance; it is a perversion of the will turning away from the Supreme Good toward lower things. If evil were a substance, it would be good.
*   **Philosophy vs. Revelation:** Neoplatonism contains the truth of God's divinity (the Word), but only Christian scripture contains the truth of His humility and saving grace (the Word made flesh).

**Notable Quotes**
> "And I enquired what iniquity was, and found it to be no substance, but the perversion of the will, turned aside from Thee, O God, the Supreme, towards these lower things..." (p. 82)

> "I had proved to be, not skilled, but killed... For where was that charity building upon the foundation of humility, which is Christ Jesus? or when should these books teach me it?" (p. 84)

### Book VIII

**Summary**
Though intellectually convinced of the Catholic faith, Augustine's will remains paralyzed by his attachment to sexual pleasure. He visits Simplicianus, who tells him the story of Victorinus, a highly respected Roman rhetorician and pagan who publicly converted to Christianity. This inspires Augustine, but he is still bound by the "chain" of his own lusts.

Later, a court official named Pontitianus visits Augustine and Alypius and casually tells them about St. Antony of Egypt and two imperial agents who instantly abandoned their secular lives to become monks upon reading Antony's life. This story throws Augustine into a severe crisis of conscience. He retreats to a garden, agonizing over his divided will. Hearing a child's voice from a neighboring house chanting "Tolle, lege" (Take up and read), he opens the Epistles of Paul to Romans 13:13-14. The verses command him to abandon the lusts of the flesh and put on Christ. His doubts instantly vanish, and he and Alypius joyfully report their conversion to Monnica.

**Key Points**
*   **The Divided Will:** Augustine introduces his profound psychological insight that his inability to convert was not due to two opposing natures (Manichaeism) but one will divided against itself by the weight of habit.
*   **The Catalyst of Exemplars:** The conversions of Victorinus and the imperial agents serve as mirrors, shaming Augustine for his hesitation despite his superior education.
*   **Divine Intervention:** The ultimate conversion is achieved not through intellectual reasoning, which was already complete, but through an act of divine grace initiated by the mysterious voice.

**Notable Quotes**
> "Thus did my two wills, one new, and the other old, one carnal, the other spiritual, struggle within me; and by their discord, undid my soul." (p. 91)

> "I seized, opened, and in silence read that section on which my eyes first fell: Not in rioting and drunkenness, not in chambering and wantonness, not in strife and envying; but put ye on the Lord Jesus Christ, and make not provision for the flesh, in concupiscence. No further would I read; nor needed I..." (p. 99-100)

### Book IX

**Summary**
Following his conversion, Augustine resolves to resign his professorship, but waits until the vintage vacation to avoid appearing ostentatious. He retreats to a country house in Cassiacum with his family and friends, writing and preparing for baptism. He, Alypius, and his son Adeodatus are baptized in Milan by Ambrose. 

The narrative then shifts to Ostia, where the group is preparing to return to Africa. Augustine shares a profound, mystical vision with his mother, Monnica, where they intellectually ascend beyond the physical universe to touch the Eternal Wisdom. Shortly after this shared ecstasy, Monnica falls ill. Having seen her life's goal (Augustine's conversion) fulfilled, she expresses no fear of dying far from home, asking only that she be remembered at the altar of the Lord. She dies, and Augustine details his struggle to suppress his human grief, ultimately weeping freely as a release and offering a prayer for her soul.

**Key Points**
*   **Retirement from the World:** Augustine peacefully severs his ties to his secular ambitions and the "marts of lip-labour."
*   **The Vision at Ostia:** A demonstration of Christian contemplation, differing from the Platonist ascent in Book VII because it is a shared, communal experience rooted in grace.
*   **Death and Christian Grief:** Augustine frames his mother's death not as a tragedy but as the completion of a holy mission, while acknowledging the complex reality of human sorrow.

**Notable Quotes**
> "And we came to our own minds, and went beyond them, that we might arrive at that region of never-failing plenty, where Thou feedest Israel for ever with the food of truth..." (p. 111)

> "Son, for mine own part I have no further delight in any thing in this life. What I do here any longer, and to what I am here, I know not, now that my hopes in this world are accomplished." (p. 112)

### Book X

**Summary**
Shifting from his past history to his present state as Bishop of Hippo, Augustine embarks on a massive philosophical exploration of memory. He marvels at the "boundless chamber" of memory, containing images, skills, emotions, and the concept of God Himself. He concludes that God transcends memory, yet it is within the mind that the search for God takes place.

Augustine then conducts a rigorous self-examination of his current vulnerabilities to sin. He reviews the temptations of the flesh, dividing them into the lust of the flesh (food, sex, smells, sounds), the lust of the eyes (curiosity and the desire for worldly knowledge), and the pride of life (the desire for human praise). He confesses that while he has conquered blatant sins, he still struggles with subtle temptations, such as eating for pleasure rather than health, or being distracted by trivial curiosities. He concludes the book by asserting that only Christ, the true Mediator, can bridge the gap between man's sinful nature and God's purity.

**Key Points**
*   **The Vastness of Memory:** Memory is the defining feature of human consciousness and identity, the space where learning, mathematics, emotions, and the search for happiness exist.
*   **Ongoing Spiritual Warfare:** Conversion is not the end of temptation. The Christian life is a continuous struggle against subtle, deeply ingrained habits of the flesh and ego.
*   **The Necessity of the True Mediator:** False mediators (like the Platonist demons) cannot save; only Christ, who shares humanity's mortality and God's righteousness, can reconcile the two.

**Notable Quotes**
> "Too late loved I Thee, O Thou Beauty of ancient days, yet ever new! too late I loved Thee! And behold, Thou wert within, and I abroad, and there I searched for Thee..." (p. 132)

> "Great is the power of memory, a fearful thing, O my God, a deep and boundless manifoldness; and this thing is the mind, and this am I myself." (p. 127)

### Book XI

**Summary**
Augustine transitions to pure theological exegesis, focusing on the opening words of Genesis: "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." He addresses the Manichaean taunt: "What was God doing before He made heaven and earth?" Augustine dismisses the joke that God was "preparing hell for pryers into mysteries" and tackles the question philosophically. 

He argues that time itself is a creation of God. Therefore, there is no "before" creation because there was no time. God dwells in a perpetual, unchangeable eternity ("the ever-fixed Eternity"), which cannot be measured against past, present, or future. Augustine then famously investigates the psychological nature of time. He concludes that the past and future do not exist in reality; they exist only in the mind as memory (past) and expectation (future). Time is a "protraction" or distension of the mind itself.

**Key Points**
*   **Eternity vs. Time:** God's eternity is an eternal present, completely outside the constraints and successions of time. Time is a feature of the created universe.
*   **The Subjective Nature of Time:** Time cannot be measured in physical space. It is a psychological phenomenon: the mind holds the past through memory, the present through attention, and the future through expectation.
*   **Human Distension:** Human existence is stretched (distended) across time, causing anxiety and loss, which can only be resolved by gathering oneself into God's eternal present.

**Notable Quotes**
> "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know: if I wish to explain it to one that asketh, I know not..." (p. 151)

> "It is in thee, my mind, that I measure times... the impression, which things as they pass by cause in thee, remains even when they are gone; this it is which still present, I measure..." (p. 159)

### Book XII

**Summary**
Continuing his exegesis of Genesis 1, Augustine focuses on the concepts of "heaven and earth," the "earth invisible and without form," and the "darksome deep." He posits the existence of two creations that are outside of time (though not coeternal with God): the "heaven of heavens" (an angelic, purely intellectual realm that perfectly contemplates God) and "formless matter" (the absolute base material of the universe, near to nothingness, waiting to receive form).

Augustine then addresses the various interpretations of Genesis proposed by different factions of Christians. Instead of arguing dogmatically for one single interpretation, he advocates for exegetical pluralism. As long as an interpretation aligns with the truth of the Catholic faith and promotes charity (love of God and neighbor), it is a valid reading. He argues that Moses, inspired by the Holy Spirit, likely intended his words to be rich enough to hold multiple true meanings.

**Key Points**
*   **The Extremes of Creation:** God created both the highest spiritual beings (the heaven of heavens) and the lowest formless matter out of nothing.
*   **Matter and Form:** Creation was not formed out of God's own substance, nor pre-existing matter, but out of absolute nothingness.
*   **Hermeneutical Charity:** Scriptural interpretation should not be a battleground of pride. Multiple interpretations of a text can be true, provided they serve the ultimate goal of love and truth.

**Notable Quotes**
> "If we both see that to be true that Thou sayest, and both see that to be true that I say, where, I pray Thee, do we see it? Neither I in thee, nor thou in me; but both in the unchangeable Truth itself, which is above our souls." (p. 175)

> "Wherefore then may I not conceive the formlessness of matter... to be suitably intimated unto men, by the name of earth invisible and without form." (p. 162)

### Book XIII

**Summary**
In the final book, Augustine interprets the entirety of the creation story in Genesis 1 as a profound allegory for the recreation and salvation of the human soul and the Church. The Holy Spirit "moving over the waters" represents God's grace lifting fallen humanity from the abyss of sin. The command "Let there be light" signifies the spiritual illumination of the soul.

He maps the days of creation onto the spiritual life: the separation of land and sea is the separation of believers from the bitter infidels; the bringing forth of fruit represents works of mercy and charity; the creation of the luminaries (sun, moon, stars) symbolizes the light of spiritual knowledge and the preaching of the Apostles; the animals represent the Sacraments and the taming of human passions. The book concludes with a meditation on the Sabbath rest—the eternal peace that awaits the faithful in God, who is the ultimate Rest.

**Key Points**
*   **The Trinity in Creation:** Augustine sees the Father as the Creator, the Son as the Beginning (Wisdom), and the Holy Spirit as the grace moving over the dark waters.
*   **Allegory of Salvation:** The physical creation mirrors the spiritual recreation of the soul from dark, formless sin to illuminated, fruit-bearing righteousness.
*   **The Ultimate Goal:** The trajectory of the *Confessions* ends where it began—seeking rest. The final paragraphs look forward to the seventh day, the eternal Sabbath where the soul will finally rest perfectly in God.

**Notable Quotes**
> "My weight, is my love; thereby am I borne, whithersoever I am borne. We are inflamed, by Thy Gift we are kindled; and are carried upwards; we glow inwardly, and go forwards." (p. 183)

> "But the seventh day hath no evening, nor hath it setting; because Thou hast sanctified it to an everlasting continuance... that we also after our works... shall rest in Thee also in the Sabbath of eternal life." (p. 201)

## Argument Arc

The book’s argument develops through a distinct three-part structure. 
1. **Books I-IX (Autobiography & History):** Augustine tracks his life from infancy to his mother's death. This section acts as the *evidence* of his theology—demonstrating original sin in childhood, the privation of evil in his rebellious adolescence, the inadequacy of secular philosophy, and the absolute necessity of divine grace for conversion. 
2. **Book X (Psychology & Present State):** A transitional, foundational chapter that shifts from past actions to present consciousness. Having documented how God saved him, Augustine analyzes the mechanism of the mind (Memory) and assesses the ongoing, present reality of temptation, proving that conversion is an ongoing process of purification rather than an instant fix.
3. **Books XI-XIII (Theology & Exegesis):** The *application* of his restored mind. Having found God and been purified, his intellect is now directed toward the highest mysteries: Time, Eternity, and the allegorical interpretation of Creation. The arc moves from the chaotic, fragmented material world of his youth into the unified, eternal, spiritual reality of the Trinity.

## Cross-Chapter Connections

*   **The Restless Search:** The thesis established in Book I ("our heart is restless") drives the narrative. Augustine tries to find rest in the theater (Book III), in a mortal friend (Book IV), in worldly ambition (Book VI), and in philosophy (Book VII). He only finds the beginning of rest in the garden conversion (Book VIII), culminating in the theological exploration of the ultimate Sabbath rest in Book XIII.
*   **The Problem of Evil:** Introduced early through his childhood beatings and the pear theft (Book II), evil is explored as an active force. Under the Manichees, he views it as a physical substance (Books III-V). Platonism later helps him redefine it as a privation of good (Book VII), setting up his understanding of his own divided will (Book VIII).
*   **The Word vs. The Flesh:** Books III-V show Augustine's obsession with eloquent *words* devoid of truth. In Book VII, he discovers the eternal *Word* (Logos) in Platonism. In Book VIII, the literal words "Take up and read" lead him to the Word made Flesh. In Book XI, he explores how God speaks creation into existence not through syllables in time, but through the eternal Word.
*   **Maternal Intercession:** The theme of Monnica's tears and prayers appears continuously. Her weeping in Book III is promised a reward. Her physical following of him to Milan (Book VI) parallels God's pursuit of his soul. Her death in Book IX represents the closing of his earthly attachments, shifting his focus entirely to the heavenly mother, the Church.
